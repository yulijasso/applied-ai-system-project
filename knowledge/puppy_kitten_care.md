# Puppy and Kitten Care Schedules

Young animals have qualitatively different needs from adults: shorter attention spans, smaller bladders, open growth plates, and developmental windows that close. A schedule tuned for an adult dog or cat is rarely appropriate for one under 12 months.

**Puppies (under ~12 months)**:
- House-training / bathroom breaks: a young puppy can hold its bladder roughly 1 hour per month of age, up to a daytime maximum of about 6–8 hours in adolescents. Always offer a break immediately after waking, after meals (~5–30 minutes), after play, and before bed. Expecting an 8-week-old to hold for 4 hours is unrealistic.
- Meals: 3–4 meals/day until ~6 months, then 2 meals/day for most breeds. Large/giant breeds typically stay on growth-formula food until 12–24 months.
- Structured exercise — the "5-minute rule" (AKC, VCA): roughly 5 minutes per month of age, once or twice daily. Examples: 3 mo → ~15 min/session; 6 mo → ~30 min; 12 mo → ~60 min.
- Avoid jogging, repetitive fetch on hard surfaces, and long hikes before growth-plate closure (typically 12–14 months small/medium breeds, 14–24 months large/giant breeds). Free play with appropriate playmates on soft surfaces is generally lower-risk than equivalent forced exercise.
- Socialization: the primary developmental window runs roughly 3–14 weeks (AVSAB, AVMA). Brief, positive exposures to varied people, surfaces, sounds, handling, and healthy/vaccinated dogs during this window reduce adult fear and aggression. AVSAB explicitly states puppies can begin socialization classes 7 days after their first set of vaccines; waiting until full vaccination is complete is too late.
- Sleep: 18–20 hours/day for young puppies; over-scheduling reliably produces overtired, reactive behavior.
- Training: short positive-reinforcement sessions of 5–10 minutes, 3–5 times per day, beat one long session.
- Vaccinations: core puppy series typically every 3–4 weeks from ~6–8 weeks through ~16 weeks, plus rabies. Final puppy boosters at ~16+ weeks are necessary because maternal antibody can block earlier doses.

**Kittens (under ~12 months)**:
- Litter box: scoop multiple times per day. Kittens have small bladders and high learning sensitivity to litter substrate and box location — early aversive experiences predict adult avoidance.
- Meals: 3–4 small meals/day until ~6 months; 2–3 meals/day from 6–12 months.
- Play: 4+ short sessions of 5–10 minutes per day, with wand/teaser toys. High prey drive without outlets often manifests as ankle-stalking and night-time zoomies.
- Sleep: 16–20 hours/day.
- Handling: brief daily gentle handling of paws, ears, and mouth in the 2–7 week sensitive period (and continued through kittenhood) builds tolerance for grooming and vet visits. Carrier desensitization started young pays off for life.
- Vaccinations: core kitten series typically every 3–4 weeks from ~6–8 weeks through ~16 weeks, plus rabies.

**Scheduling implications**:
- A 3-month-old puppy on a 60-minute structured walk is well above the 5-minute-rule range and is a developmental risk; the size-matched session is roughly 15 minutes.
- A young puppy with bathroom breaks scheduled 4+ hours apart during the day is below the breaks-per-hour-of-age guideline and likely to fail house-training.
- A single 5-minute play session per day for a kitten is below typical play-need; 4+ sessions is the AAFP-aligned target.

Source: AVMA, "Socialization of dogs and cats" and the 2024 AVMA literature review on puppy/kitten socialization (avma.org); AVSAB Position Statement on Puppy Socialization (avsab.org); AKC Expert Advice — "Puppy Socialization," "How Much Exercise Does a Puppy Need?", and "Puppy Schedule" (akc.org); VCA Animal Hospitals — "Exercise Guidelines For Puppies" and "Puppy Behavior and Training: Socialization and Fear Prevention" (vcahospitals.com); AAFP/ISFM Feline Life Stage Guidelines (catvets.com).
