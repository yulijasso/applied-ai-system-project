# Cat Enrichment and Indoor Activity Needs

Indoor cats need predictable opportunities to express predatory, exploratory, and resting behaviors. Inadequate enrichment is associated with obesity, lower urinary tract signs (Pandora syndrome / FIC), inter-cat aggression, and inappropriate elimination. The AAFP/ISFM Feline Environmental Needs framework organizes these needs into five "pillars": safe space, multiple separated key resources, opportunity to play and predatory behavior, positive human social interaction, and respect for the cat's sense of smell.

**Interactive play (predatory simulation)**:
- Adult cats (1–10 years): at least 2–3 short play sessions per day, roughly 10 minutes each, using wand/teaser toys that mimic hunt-stalk-pounce-bite-consume sequences. Dawn and dusk are the most receptive windows.
- Kittens (under 1 year): 4 or more short sessions of 5–10 minutes; play drive is high and frustration builds quickly without outlets.
- Senior/geriatric cats (10+ years): 1–2 gentler sessions of 5–10 minutes with low-impact toys; arthritis is present in roughly 60–90% of cats over 12 and limits jumping/lunging.
- Session length should taper when the cat disengages or pants; ending on a "successful catch" reduces frustration.

**Other forms that count toward enrichment**:
- Food puzzles or foraging toys delivering part of the daily ration (slows eating, gives cognitive work).
- Vertical territory: cat trees, shelves, window perches with outdoor sightlines.
- Scent enrichment: catnip, silver vine, valerian (rotated; ~30–50% of cats do not respond to catnip).
- Solo toys rotated every few days — cats habituate to a static toy within days.

**What does not substitute for interactive play**:
- Petting and lap time (companionship, not predatory outlet).
- Toys left permanently on the floor without rotation.
- Another cat in the household — co-housed cats often need their own play time, not less.

**Resource separation (multi-cat homes)**: food, water, litter, resting, and scratching resources should be provided in multiple locations so a cat can access them without crossing another cat's path. The "n+1" rule applies to litter boxes (see litter_box_hygiene.md) and is good practice for food/water stations as well.

**Scheduling implications**: a single short play session per day is below the AAFP-aligned target for an adult or young cat; total interactive play under ~20 minutes/day for a non-senior cat is on the low side. For seniors, frequency matters more than session length.

Source: AAFP/ISFM 2013 Feline Environmental Needs Guidelines (catvets.com/resource/aafp-isfm-environmental-needs-guidelines); AAFP Environment Enhancement of Indoor Cats Position Statement; Ohio State Indoor Pet Initiative (indoorpet.osu.edu/cats); International Cat Care, "Playing with your cat" (icatcare.org); VCA Animal Hospitals, "Enrichment for Indoor Cats."
