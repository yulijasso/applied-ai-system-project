# Grooming Frequency by Coat Type

Grooming cadence is driven by coat type and lifestyle, not breed name alone. Two dogs of the same breed can have meaningfully different needs based on coat condition and outdoor exposure.

**Brushing — typical frequency by coat type (AKC, VCA grooming guides)**:
- Double-coated breeds (Golden Retriever, Labrador, Husky, German Shepherd, Bernese Mountain Dog, Pomeranian, Akita): daily brushing during the spring and fall seasonal "blow," and 2–3 times per week otherwise. Mats build behind ears, on the chest, and around the rear; shaving a healthy double coat is generally discouraged because it can disrupt thermoregulation.
- Long single-coat breeds (Yorkie, Maltese, Shih Tzu, Afghan Hound): daily brushing is typical — these coats mat within roughly 24–48 hours when neglected.
- Curly / non-shedding coats (Poodle, Bichon, most Doodle crosses): brush every 1–3 days plus professional grooming every 4–8 weeks. Mats are common and often require shave-downs if left.
- Short smooth coats (Beagle, Boxer, Lab, Bulldog, Pointer): 1–2 times per week is generally sufficient; weekly is enough for most.
- Wire coats (most terriers, Schnauzer): weekly brushing; hand-stripping every 8–12 weeks for show coats, periodic clipping for pet coats.
- Cats: short-haired cats benefit from weekly brushing; long-haired cats (Persian, Maine Coon, Ragdoll) need daily or near-daily brushing to prevent mats and reduce hairball burden.

**Bathing — typical frequency**:
- Most healthy dogs: roughly every 4–8 weeks. Non-shedding curly coats often go 6–8 weeks between baths/grooms. Over-bathing strips skin oils and can trigger irritation.
- Active outdoor dogs, swimmers, or dogs with skin conditions: as directed by the veterinarian; medicated baths can be weekly or twice weekly during a flare.
- Cats: routine bathing is generally unnecessary — cats self-groom. Bathing is reserved for soiling, hairless breeds (Sphynx-type, every 1–2 weeks), or medical indications.

**Nail trims**: every 3–4 weeks for most pets; nails clicking on a hard floor while the dog is standing relaxed indicates they are overgrown. Senior or sedentary pets often need more frequent trims because they wear down nails less through activity.

**Ears**: weekly check; clean only if visibly dirty or as directed for breeds prone to otitis (drop-eared breeds, swimmers, Cockers, retrievers with allergies).

**Anal glands**: not a routine grooming task for most dogs; only address if scooting, licking the area, or a fishy odor appears. Routine elective expression in asymptomatic dogs is not recommended by most veterinary dermatologists.

**Scheduling implications**: two grooming sessions in a single day for the same pet is generally excessive unless they are different tasks (e.g., a quick brush-out and a separate teeth-brushing). For curly-coated dogs, missed brushing days compound quickly — a 4–6 day gap often produces mats requiring professional attention.

Source: AKC Expert Advice grooming guides — "How Should You Groom a Double-Coated Dog?", "How Often Should You Bathe Your Dog?" (akc.org); VCA Animal Hospitals, "Grooming and Coat Care for Your Dog" and "Coat and Skin Appearance in the Healthy Dog" (vcahospitals.com); ASPCA Dog Grooming Tips (aspca.org).
