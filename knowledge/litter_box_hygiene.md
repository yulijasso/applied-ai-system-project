# Litter Box Hygiene and Cleaning Frequency

Litter-box dissatisfaction is one of the most common drivers of inappropriate elimination in cats, which is in turn the leading behavioral reason cats are surrendered to shelters. Hygiene, location, and number of boxes interact — fixing only one rarely solves the problem.

**Cleaning cadence**:
- Scoop at least once daily; many cats prefer twice-daily scooping. A box scooped less than once per day correlates with avoidance behavior in surveys of indoor cats.
- Full litter change and box wash: roughly weekly for clumping clay litter; every 2–4 weeks is acceptable for crystal/silica or pellet systems used as labeled.
- Wash with warm water and unscented mild soap (e.g., dish soap). Avoid bleach, ammonia (smells like cat urine and discourages return to the box), citrus, pine-scented, and other strongly perfumed cleaners.
- Replace plastic boxes annually or whenever the surface becomes scratched and porous — pitted plastic holds odor that scrubbing cannot remove.

**Number of boxes — the "n+1" rule (AAFP/ISFM; ASPCA; OSU Indoor Pet Initiative)**:
- One box per cat plus one extra (1 cat → 2 boxes; 2 cats → 3 boxes; 3 cats → 4 boxes).
- In multi-story homes, place at least one box on each floor the cat regularly uses; cats with arthritis or kittens may not negotiate stairs reliably to reach a single box.

**Box and location characteristics**:
- Box size: roughly 1.5 times the length of the cat from nose to base of tail. Many commercial boxes are too small for adult cats; storage totes are commonly used as larger alternatives.
- Most cats prefer uncovered boxes with unscented, fine-grained clumping litter (multiple preference studies).
- Place in quiet, low-traffic but not isolated locations; cats want a clear sightline and a way out (avoid dead-end closets).
- Keep boxes away from food and water bowls — cats avoid eliminating near food.
- Avoid placement next to noisy appliances (washer, dryer, furnace) that may startle the cat mid-use.

**Indicators of inadequate hygiene or fit**:
- Elimination just outside the box, on adjacent surfaces, or on soft items like rugs and laundry.
- Hovering or balancing on the rim rather than stepping in.
- Long pre-elimination digging followed by rushing out.
- Vocalization on entry/exit (this can also be urinary tract pain — a single new instance warrants a vet visit, especially in male cats, where urethral obstruction is a true emergency).

**Senior or arthritic cats**: low-entry boxes (entry height ~2–3 inches) or a box with one side cut down. High-walled or top-entry boxes can become inaccessible to cats with osteoarthritis, which is present in roughly 60–90% of cats over 12.

**Scheduling implications**: a daily "scoop litter" task is the appropriate cadence; less frequent scooping is below standard of care and is a predictable trigger for house-soiling.

Source: AAFP/ISFM 2013 Feline Environmental Needs Guidelines (catvets.com); ASPCA, "Litter Box Problems" (aspca.org); Cornell Feline Health Center materials (vet.cornell.edu); Ohio State Indoor Pet Initiative, "Litter Boxes" (indoorpet.osu.edu/cats/basic-indoor-cat-needs/litter-boxes); VCA Animal Hospitals, "Housetraining for Kittens and Cats" (vcahospitals.com).
