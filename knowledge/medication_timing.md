# Medication Timing Rules for Pets

Many common pet medications have timing constraints that interact with meals, other drugs, and exercise. The rules below are general — a specific prescription label always overrides them.

**Give with food (during the meal or within ~30 minutes)**:
- NSAIDs — carprofen (Rimadyl), meloxicam (Metacam), deracoxib, firocoxib, robenacoxib, grapiprant. Empty-stomach dosing is a common cause of vomiting and is a known risk for GI ulceration. (Cornell NSAID guide; VCA carprofen/meloxicam monographs.)
- Macrocyclic-lactone heartworm chews (ivermectin in Heartgard, milbemycin in Interceptor/Sentinel) — all are designed as flavored chews and absorption of the fat-soluble drug is improved with a small fatty meal.
- Metronidazole — reduces nausea.
- Most oral corticosteroids (prednisone, prednisolone) — reduces GI irritation; long-term use also benefits from a gastroprotectant.
- Cyclosporine for dogs with atopic dermatitis is the exception — it is given on an empty stomach for predictable absorption.

**Give on an empty stomach (typically 1+ hour before or 2+ hours after food)**:
- Levothyroxine (thyroid replacement) — food reduces and varies absorption. Most veterinarians recommend giving consistently the same way each day; if the dog must have it with food, dose monitoring is adjusted accordingly. (VCA; Cornell hypothyroidism page; Merck Vet Manual.)
- Sucralfate — must coat an empty stomach; wait ~1 hour before food or other oral medications.
- Some antibiotics (ampicillin, penicillin V) — food reduces absorption modestly.

**Special handling for cats — doxycycline and other tablets/capsules**:
- Cat esophagi do not clear pills well; doxycycline tablets and capsules are a recognized cause of esophageal stricture. Always follow a pill with at least 6 mL of water by syringe, or with a small bolus of food, so the pill enters the stomach. In one study, only ~37% of dry-given pills had reached the stomach at 5 minutes; with a water chaser, ~100% had reached the stomach within 60 seconds.

**Spacing between medications**:
- Antacids (famotidine, omeprazole) and oral antibiotics: separate by at least ~2 hours where possible — gastric pH affects absorption of some antibiotics.
- Calcium-, magnesium-, aluminum-, or iron-containing supplements/antacids and tetracycline-class antibiotics (doxycycline, minocycline): separate by ~2 hours; chelation reduces antibiotic absorption.
- Probiotics and oral antibiotics: separate by ~2 hours so the antibiotic does not kill the probiotic dose.

**Insulin (diabetic dogs and cats)**:
- Inject with or immediately after a meal. Standard regimens are twice daily, ~12 hours apart.
- If the pet refuses to eat, the standard owner instruction is to give a partial dose or skip and contact the veterinary team rather than injecting a full dose into a fasted patient — hypoglycemia is the most common diabetic emergency.

**Scheduling implications**:
- An NSAID scheduled before a meal, or 30+ minutes before a walk on an empty stomach, is in the wrong order; move the dose to the meal.
- A levothyroxine dose stacked next to breakfast is suboptimal; either give well before the meal or commit to consistent with-food dosing.
- A doxycycline dose given dry to a cat (no water or food chaser) is a flagged risk pattern.

Source: Cornell University Pharmacy NSAID medication guide (vet.cornell.edu); VCA Animal Hospitals drug monographs — carprofen, meloxicam, levothyroxine, doxycycline, sucralfate (vcahospitals.com); Merck Veterinary Manual, "Nonsteroidal Anti-inflammatory Drugs in Animals" and "Tetracyclines Use in Animals" (merckvetmanual.com); American Heartworm Society preventives page (heartwormsociety.org).
