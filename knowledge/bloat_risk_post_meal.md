# Bloat (Gastric Dilatation-Volvulus) Risk and Meal Timing

Gastric Dilatation-Volvulus (GDV), commonly called "bloat," is a rapidly fatal condition in which the stomach distends with gas/fluid and rotates on its axis. Mortality without surgery approaches 100%; with prompt surgery, reported mortality is roughly 15–33%.

**Highest-risk breeds (deep, narrow chest conformation)**: Great Dane, Saint Bernard, Weimaraner, Irish Setter, Gordon Setter, Standard Poodle, German Shepherd, Doberman, Akita, Bloodhound, Newfoundland, Irish Wolfhound. Lifetime incidence in Great Danes has been estimated near 37%. Labrador and Golden Retrievers are lower-risk than these breeds but higher-risk than the average dog. Small/toy breeds are at very low risk.

**Established risk factors (Glickman et al., JAVMA 2000; Cornell Riney Canine Health Center)**:
- First-degree relative with a history of GDV
- Older age (risk rises notably after 7 years in large breeds, 5 years in giant breeds)
- Eating one large meal per day rather than multiple smaller meals
- Rapid eating
- Raised/elevated food bowls — attributed to ~20% of cases in large breeds and ~52% in giant breeds in the cited study
- Lean body condition; anxious or fearful temperament
- Dry-food-only diets where one of the first four ingredients is an oil/fat

**Activity around meals (at-risk breeds)**:
- Vigorous exercise (running, fetch, off-leash play) within roughly 1 hour before and 1–2 hours after a large meal is associated with higher GDV risk in deep-chested breeds.
- Slow leashed walking near meal time has not been clearly linked to GDV and is generally considered acceptable.
- Large-volume water intake immediately followed by vigorous activity is a recognized trigger.

**Scheduling implications for at-risk breeds**:
- A plan that places a run, fetch session, or long brisk walk inside the 1-hour-before / 1–2-hour-after meal window is a recognizable GDV risk pattern worth flagging.
- Splitting the daily ration into 2–3 meals lowers risk versus a single large meal.
- For confirmed at-risk dogs, prophylactic gastropexy (often combined with spay/neuter) is the only intervention shown to substantially reduce mortality.

**Early signs (emergency)**: unproductive retching, distended/tympanic abdomen, restlessness, excessive drooling, pale gums, collapse. These warrant immediate emergency veterinary care; GDV progresses to shock within hours.
