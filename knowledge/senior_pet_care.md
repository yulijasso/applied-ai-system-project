# Senior Pet Care Considerations

The 2023 AAHA Senior Care Guidelines define "senior" as the last roughly 25% of a pet's expected lifespan, which is breed- and size-dependent rather than a single age threshold.

**Approximate senior age ranges**:
- Small-breed dogs (under ~20 lb): senior around 10–12 years; many remain active well past this.
- Medium-breed dogs: senior around 8–10 years.
- Large-breed dogs (50–90 lb): senior around 7–8 years.
- Giant breeds (Great Dane, Mastiff, Saint Bernard, Irish Wolfhound): senior as early as 5–6 years.
- Cats: senior at 10+ years; "geriatric" at 15+ (AAFP/AAHA Feline Life Stage Guidelines).

**Schedule adjustments common in senior pets**:
- Walks: shift to shorter, more frequent sessions — for dogs, 2–3 walks of 15–25 minutes often suit aging joints better than a single long walk. Surfaces matter: grass, dirt, and rubberized paths are easier on osteoarthritic joints than concrete and asphalt.
- Avoid high-impact bursts (long sprints, repeated jumps from heights, repetitive ball-fetch on hard surfaces) when osteoarthritis is suspected; OA is present in the majority of dogs over 8 and roughly 60–90% of cats over 12.
- Cold weather: senior pets thermoregulate less efficiently. Outdoor time in winter for short-coated, lean, or arthritic dogs is shorter, and a coat is often warranted at temperatures below ~40°F / 4°C.
- Bathroom breaks: senior dogs often need 4+ outdoor trips per day rather than 2–3; nocturia is common with CKD, diabetes, and Cushing's.
- Mealtimes: allow extra time and a quiet space; rushed feedings can worsen poor appetite. For dogs with vestibular disease or cervical pain, slightly elevated bowls may help — note this is the opposite of guidance for at-risk young large-breed dogs (see bloat_risk_post_meal.md).

**Common senior conditions affecting daily scheduling**:
- Osteoarthritis: the most common chronic condition in senior dogs and cats. NSAID dosing is typically once daily with food (see medication_timing.md). Multimodal management — weight control, joint supplements (omega-3s, polysulfated glycosaminoglycans), monoclonal antibodies (e.g., Solensia for cats, Librela for dogs), gentle daily exercise — outperforms pain medication alone.
- Cognitive dysfunction syndrome (CDS): consistent daily routines reduce nighttime restlessness and confusion. Avoid sudden schedule, furniture, or feeding-location changes.
- Chronic kidney disease (especially cats): multiple small meals, water available in several locations, possible at-home subcutaneous fluids on a fixed schedule.
- Diabetes mellitus: strict twice-daily meal-and-insulin pairing (see feeding_schedule.md and medication_timing.md).
- Hyperthyroidism (cats): twice-daily methimazole or once-daily transdermal; weight and appetite trend monitoring matter as much as the dose.
- Vision/hearing loss: keep furniture, food, water, and litter locations stable; approach the pet from the front and announce yourself before contact.

**Veterinary visit cadence**: AAHA recommends senior pets have wellness exams every 6 months (versus annually for younger adults), with bloodwork and urinalysis at least annually and often twice yearly to catch CKD, diabetes, hyperthyroidism, and liver disease earlier than symptoms appear.

**Scheduling implications**: a senior pet's plan that mirrors adult-pet activity — single long walk, vigorous fetch, irregular meal timing — is below standard for senior care. Shorter, more frequent, lower-impact sessions and a stable daily rhythm are the default.

Source: 2023 AAHA Senior Care Guidelines for Dogs and Cats (aaha.org/resources/2023-aaha-senior-care-guidelines-for-dogs-and-cats); 2019 AAHA Canine Life Stage Guidelines (aaha.org); AAFP/AAHA Feline Life Stage Guidelines (catvets.com); Cornell Feline Health Center senior cat resources (vet.cornell.edu); VCA Animal Hospitals, "Feeding Mature, Senior, and Geriatric Cats" (vcahospitals.com).
