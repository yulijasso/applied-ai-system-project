# Dog Exercise Needs by Breed and Life Stage

Daily exercise targets are highly breed- and age-dependent. The bullets below describe typical ranges; deviation in either direction is a flag worth checking against the dog's specific profile.

**Healthy adult dogs (roughly 1–7 years for most breeds; 1–5 years for giant breeds)**:
- High-energy working/sporting breeds (Border Collie, Australian Shepherd, Belgian Malinois, Vizsla, Weimaraner, German Shorthaired Pointer, Husky, Labrador, Golden Retriever, Standard Poodle): typical target is 60–120 minutes/day of active exercise, ideally split across two or more sessions and including off-leash running or scent/agility work where safe. Daily activity under ~60 minutes for these breeds frequently produces destructive or anxiety behaviors.
- Moderate-energy breeds (Beagle, Cocker Spaniel, Boxer, most terriers, Corgi): typical target 45–60 minutes/day.
- Low-energy or brachycephalic breeds (Bulldog, French Bulldog, Pug, Boston Terrier, Pekingese, Basset Hound, Shih Tzu): typical target 20–30 minutes/day, split into shorter walks in cool parts of the day. Brachycephalic dogs cool inefficiently through panting and tire faster — see heat_safety_walking.md.

**Puppies under ~12 months (the "5-minute rule" of thumb, AKC/VCA)**:
- Roughly 5 minutes of structured leashed exercise per month of age, up to twice daily. Examples: 3 months → ~15 min/session; 6 months → ~30 min/session; 12 months → ~60 min/session.
- Free play with appropriate playmates on soft surfaces is generally lower-risk than forced/structured exercise of the same duration.
- Jogging, repetitive fetch on hard surfaces, and long hikes are not recommended before growth-plate closure — typically 12–14 months in small/medium breeds and 14–24 months in large/giant breeds. Premature high-impact exercise is associated with developmental orthopedic disease.

**Senior dogs (last ~25% of expected lifespan — roughly 7+ years for medium breeds, 5+ for giant breeds, 10–12+ for small breeds)**:
- Typical target 20–40 minutes/day of low-impact activity, often split into 2–3 shorter walks. Joint-friendly surfaces (grass, dirt, sand) are preferable to repetitive concrete.
- High-intensity bursts (long fetch sessions, sprinting, jumping from heights) are commonly contraindicated when osteoarthritis is present, which it is in the majority of dogs over 8.

**Mental exercise counts toward overall need**: scent games, food puzzles, training sessions, and novel environments reduce the physical-exercise gap, particularly for high-drive breeds confined indoors.

**Size-variant breeds**: when a breed family spans multiple sizes, classify by the size — each size typically falls in a different energy bucket above. AKC and VCA breed pages give qualitative energy descriptors (and a few specific numbers), not minute-by-minute targets per size, so map each size to the appropriate bucket above rather than inventing a unique range.
- Poodle: Standard Poodle is in the high-energy bucket (originally bred as a water retriever; AKC describes Standards as enjoying fetch, jogging, swimming, and other vigorous activity). Miniature Poodle is in the moderate-energy bucket — VCA: "A walk around the block once or twice daily, plus a vigorous game in the yard, will meet its exercise needs." Toy Poodle is in the low-energy bucket (20–30 min/day target) — VCA classifies the Toy Poodle as "Lap Dog 5/5, Jogging Partner 2/5" and writes that "a daily walk around the block, coupled with several play sessions, will meet its exercise needs."
- Schnauzer: Giant Schnauzer is in the high-energy bucket (working group; AKC notes they require "considerable physical exercise" to prevent destructiveness). Standard Schnauzer is in the moderate-energy bucket (working group, lower than Giant but still active). Miniature Schnauzer is in the moderate-energy bucket — AKC specifically: "content with an hour of physical activity daily."
- Spaniel: English Springer Spaniel is in the high-energy bucket (sporting bird dog with great stamina). Cocker Spaniel and English Cocker Spaniel are in the moderate-energy bucket (already listed in the moderate bullet). Cavalier King Charles Spaniel is in the moderate-energy bucket (45–60 min/day target) — VCA explicitly classifies the breed's exercise level as "Moderate" and notes it "needs to walk or play every day"; AKC describes Cavaliers as combining "the gentle attentiveness of a toy breed with the verve and athleticism of a sporting spaniel."
- Dachshund (standard or miniature): moderate-energy bucket, with a critical caveat — Dachshunds (and other long-back / short-leg breeds) are at high risk of intervertebral disc disease (IVDD). AKC and VCA specifically advise restricting jumping off furniture, repeated stairs, and high-impact play regardless of total daily duration. Hansen type I disc disease occurs most commonly in younger Dachshunds 3–6 years of age.

**Disambiguating an unspecified breed name**: when the pet description lists only the family name without a size qualifier (e.g., "Poodle" rather than "Standard Poodle"), evaluate the plan against every plausible size variant and only flag it as out-of-range if it falls outside the union of those variants' ranges. A 60-minute walk for an unspecified "Poodle" is in-range for a Standard Poodle (high-energy bucket, 60–120 min/day), so it should not be shortened — even though the same duration would exceed what's typical for a Toy Poodle. The same logic applies to "Schnauzer", "Spaniel", and any other multi-variant family name.

**How to apply this when judging a plan**: identify the matching breed-energy bucket (and size variant when relevant) and the life stage, then compare the planned daily total to that bucket's range. The same 20-minute walk that is below target for a 3-year-old Border Collie is appropriate for a 2-year-old French Bulldog and on the higher end of the range for a 13-year-old Pug.
