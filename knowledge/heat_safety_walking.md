# Hot Weather and Walking Safety

Heat stroke is one of the leading preventable summer emergencies in dogs and is fatal in roughly 50% of clinical cases when severe. Outdoor activity decisions should be driven by ambient temperature, humidity, sun exposure, and the individual dog's risk profile — not the time on a clock alone.

**Ambient air-temperature ranges (general guidance for healthy adult dogs; humidity, direct sun, and pavement raise effective heat substantially)**:
- Below ~70°F / 21°C: generally safe for any healthy dog at typical exercise intensity.
- ~70–80°F / 21–27°C: generally safe; brachycephalic, senior, overweight, dark-coated, or cardio/respiratory-compromised dogs need shorter outings and watchful monitoring.
- ~80–85°F / 27–29°C: walks shorter than usual, in shade, with frequent water breaks; high-intensity exercise (running, fetch) is not advisable for at-risk dogs in this range.
- ~85–90°F / 29–32°C: brief outings only — typically a 5–15 minute potty/sniff walk in shaded routes for healthy dogs; brachycephalic and senior dogs are usually best kept indoors.
- Above ~90°F / 32°C, or with high humidity at lower temps: heat stroke risk is elevated for any dog; indoor-only enrichment is the safer choice. AVMA and ASPCA guidance specifically warns that heat stroke is possible any time temperature is above 80°F or humidity is high.

**Pavement check (paw burns are common in summer)**:
- Asphalt and dark concrete can be 40–60°F hotter than air temperature. At an air temperature of 77°F, asphalt has been measured at 125°F (AKC, "Protect Dog Paws From Hot Pavement").
- The "7-second hand test": if you cannot comfortably hold the back of your hand on the surface for 7 seconds, it is too hot for paws. Grass, dirt, and shaded paths are dramatically cooler.

**Higher-risk groups in heat (lower thresholds apply)**:
- Brachycephalic breeds (Bulldog, French Bulldog, Pug, Boston Terrier, Boxer, Pekingese) — anatomically inefficient at panting-based cooling; clinical heat stroke can occur at "only moderately elevated" temperatures (Merck Veterinary Manual; AAHA).
- Dark-coated dogs in direct sun (higher solar absorption).
- Senior dogs, puppies under ~6 months, and overweight dogs.
- Dogs with laryngeal paralysis, tracheal collapse, or any chronic cardiorespiratory disease.

**Best-practice timing in hot months**:
- Walk before ~8 a.m. or after ~7 p.m., when both air and pavement have cooled.
- Carry water and offer it every 10–15 minutes during exertion.
- Keep routes shaded; choose grass over asphalt; never leave a dog in a parked car (interior temperatures rise ~20°F in 10 minutes even with windows cracked).

**Heat stroke is a medical emergency. Early signs**: heavy panting that does not slow at rest, bright red or muddy gums, thick ropy drool, wobbliness, vomiting/diarrhea (sometimes bloody), collapse. **First aid en route to a vet**: move to shade, wet with cool (not ice-cold) water, especially under the legs and belly, and run a fan over the wet coat. Aggressive ice baths are no longer recommended — they cause peripheral vasoconstriction and slow core cooling.

**Scheduling implications**: a midday outdoor walk above ~85°F, or above ~80°F for a brachycephalic/senior/overweight dog, falls outside safe-walking guidance and is appropriate to flag for rescheduling to early morning or evening, or to substitute with indoor enrichment.

Source: AVMA, "Warm weather pet safety" and "Walking with your pet" (avma.org); ASPCA Hot Weather Safety Tips (aspca.org); AAHA, "Heat safety warnings" and "Walking on Sunshine" (aaha.org); AKC Expert Advice, "How to Protect Dog Paws From Hot Pavement" and "Heatstroke in Dogs" (akc.org); Merck Veterinary Manual, heat stroke and brachycephalic syndrome entries (merckvetmanual.com); VCA, "Heat Stroke in Dogs" (vcahospitals.com).
