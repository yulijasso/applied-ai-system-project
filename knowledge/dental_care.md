# Dental Care for Dogs and Cats

Periodontal disease is the most common clinical condition in adult dogs and cats. Roughly 80%+ of dogs and a majority of cats show signs of periodontal disease by age 3 (AVMA, AAHA 2019 Dental Care Guidelines). Most disease is subgingival and not visible on the crown.

**Tooth brushing (the only home measure proven to control plaque on its own)**:
- Daily brushing is the gold standard; plaque begins mineralizing into calculus within ~24–72 hours, so intervals longer than ~3 days lose most of the benefit.
- Brushing 3 times per week is the minimum threshold at which a measurable plaque-control effect has been demonstrated; less frequent than that is closer to no brushing in published outcomes.
- Session length of roughly 30 seconds to 2 minutes is typical; focus on the buccal (cheek-side) surfaces, where plaque accumulates most heavily.
- Use only veterinary-formulated toothpaste. Human toothpaste is contraindicated — fluoride is toxic if swallowed, and any product containing xylitol can cause life-threatening hypoglycemia in dogs.

**Adjuncts that supplement (do not replace) brushing — choose products bearing the VOHC Seal of Acceptance**:
- Dental diets (e.g., Hill's Prescription Diet t/d, Royal Canin Dental) — VOHC-accepted as a complete diet.
- Dental chews / treats (e.g., Greenies, OraVet) — daily use as labeled.
- Water additives, oral gels, dental wipes — useful where brushing is refused; effect sizes are smaller than brushing.

**Professional dental cleanings under general anesthesia (COHAT)**:
- Frequency is individualized; many dogs and cats need a COHAT every 1–2 years starting in young adulthood, with small breeds and cats often needing it sooner due to crowding and feline tooth resorption.
- Dental radiographs are part of the procedure — the AVMA and AAHA note that a substantial fraction of clinically significant pathology is subgingival and missed without imaging.
- "Anesthesia-free" dental cleaning is opposed by the American Veterinary Dental College and AAHA: it scales only visible crown surfaces, cannot probe, cannot image subgingivally, and risks aspiration and oral injury.

**Signs that warrant a veterinary exam (not a "wait and see")**:
- Halitosis (the most common early sign).
- Visible tartar at the gumline; red, swollen, or bleeding gums.
- Drooling, reluctance to chew kibble, dropping food, chewing on one side.
- Pawing at the mouth, head shyness, facial swelling. Facial swelling below an eye in a dog is suggestive of a tooth-root abscess.
- In cats: any of the above plus chattering of the jaw on contact (suggestive of feline tooth resorption).

**Scheduling implications**: a daily "brush teeth" recurrence is the appropriate cadence; a long-term plan that contains no oral-care task at all is below standard of care for a pet older than ~6 months.

Source: AVMA, "Pet dental care" (avma.org/resources-tools/pet-owners/petcare/pet-dental-care); 2019 AAHA Dental Care Guidelines for Dogs and Cats (aaha.org); American Veterinary Dental College, position on anesthesia-free dentistry (avdc.org); Veterinary Oral Health Council accepted-products lists (vohc.org); Cornell Riney Canine Health Center, "Dental disease and home dental care" (vet.cornell.edu).
